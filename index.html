Beqa Lagoon, Fiji

An ENVS 17: Marine Policy Place-Based Project by Isabel Prado-Tucker

Off the Sourthern shore of Fiji’s main island, Viti Levu, lies the Beqa Lagoon. Beqa Lagoon is a tourist hotspot, especially for shark diving. In 2010, 7.9 million dollars in revenue was generated by the two shark diving tourism operators in the area.1 The Shark Corridor is a 30km stretch of protected water within the lagoon where shark fishing is prohibited.1 Within the Shark Corridor, the main diving operator, Beqa Adventure Divers (BAD), runs 2 dives a day, 5 days a week in the Shark Reef Marine Reserve (SRMR), that they established as Fiji’s first National Marine Park in 2014. BAD partners with the nearby village of Galoa to manage the Marine Park and promote a blue economy.

Shark Reef Marine Reserve


Species Protected

Bull sharks, grey reef sharks, whitetip and blacktip reef sharks, tawny nurse sharks, sicklefin lemon sharks, silvertip sharks, and tiger sharks.

Qoliqoli Rights

The Quliqoli system grants fishing rights to Fijian coastal communities for subsistence fishing within their customary fishing rights areas. Village chiefs must approve any commercial fishing within their Qoliqoli area before it can be endorsed by the government. The village of Galoa sold their Qoliqoli rights to their part of the Beqa Lagoon to Beqa Adventure divers, to create the no-take zone Shark Ree Marine Reserve.1

Levy Payments

A $10.80 USD entrance fee is collected from anyone who enters the Shark Reef Marine Reserve, which is paid to the villages who sold their Qoliqoli rights. Roughly $100,000 is distributed to local communities each year, and the money goes towards various projects including construction, education, infastructure development, and flood mitigation.1

Community Participation

Beqa Adventure Divers are in charge of enforcing the Marine Protected Area. In partnership with the Fiji Department of Fisheries, they train their staff and local fishermen to become fish wardens, representatives of the Dept. who report and document illegal fishing activities to prompt law enforcement agencies to investigate. In addition, the majority of Beqa Adventure Divers employees are locals as they train one individual from Galoa each year to become a divemaster.1

History of the Shark Reef Marine Reserve

2004

Shark Reef Marine Reserve established by Beqa Adventure Divers in partnership with the village of Galoa and the Fiji Ministry of Fisheries.2

2009

Beqa Adventure Divers produces Fiji's first Shark Conservation PSA.2

2011

Beqa Adventure Divers plants 330,000 mangrove trees throughout the Navua River system, becoming the world's first carbon neutral dive operator.2

2012-2016

Great Fiji Shark Count takes place, a citizen science project in collaboration with the Fijian government, NGOs, and dive tourism operators to count sharks at dive sites throughout the country.3

2014

Shark Reef Marine Reserve officially becomes Fiji's first Marine Park, allowing the no-take zone to be enforced.2

2017

At the UN Ocean Conference, Fiji supports Sustainable Development Goal 14: Life Below Water by commiting to conserve all shark and ray species, as well as their habitats, in Fijian waters.2

2020-2021

The COVID-19 pandemic increases poaching and overfishing in the Beqa Lagoon by subsistence fishermen.4

2022

Coral bleaching event from sustained average daily temperatures over 29 degrees celsius for over 10 weeks. Coral cover has not significantly decreased, but there's an increase in Acropora corals (elkhorn, staghorn, etc.) which are more suseptible to bleaching and storm damage.4

Present-

Galoa Village, Beqa Adventure Divers, Fiji’s Ministry of Fisheries, the United Nations Development Programme, and various NGOs continue to collaborate to support local communities, protect the Beqa Lagoon, conserve sharks, and conduct research to understand the effects of shark tourism on the marine ecosystem.
